Our Story

Camden Dairy Farm  has a long history of being a part of its communities, and it lives up to its reputation as a significant corporation involved with the dairy business. The local support for its products demonstrates the value Camden Dairy Farm presents for them. It is a mutual relationship in which people remain commercially and socially connected to the business.

For the past 127 years, the Camden Dairy Farm members have come together with the shared goal of having their milk produce marketed. Over its long history, this historic farmer-owned dairy co-operative has developed itself numerous times, giving it a powerful example of really resilient and sustainable company.

F.A.Q

We've invited our dairy farmers, veterinarians and other experts to provide real answers to commonly asked questions about dairy farming, cows and the environment.

  • How much milk does one cow can produce each day?

    The majority of dairy cows are milked twice or three times every day. A cow will make 6-10 litres of milk each day on average.

  • Do cows get milk year round?

    No! Cows start producing milk after giving birth to a calf. Most dairy cows have their first calf at the age of two years. They are milked for about 9 or 10 months after giving birth, followed by a 2 to 3 month rest period until they give birth to their second calf. This hiatus, often known as a "dry period," is required to keep the cow and her udder healthy. Cows, like humans, are pregnant for nine months before giving birth.

  • What do you do when a cow gets sick?

    Cows, like humans, get sick and need medicine from time to time. Antibiotics are only prescribed by large-animal vets when absolutely necessary. When a cow receives medication, her milk is removed from the market and does not enter the food supply until the medication withdrawal time has passed. Sick cows are often housed separately from the herd so that they can be closely observed, and they wear leg bands to prevent them from being milked with the rest of the herd.

  • What do cows eat?

    Colostrum (a mother's first milk) is fed to newborn calves within the first 24 hours after life to ensure they acquire all of the antibodies, nutrients, and minerals they need for a good start. They will drink milk or a specially made milk substitute for the next 2-3 months. They can also get fresh grain and water. Calves are weaned off milk after 2-3 months and provided a diet of grain, hay, and water. Calves, like most youngsters, are finicky, thus most calf grain is coated in molasses to make it sweeter and taste better.

  • Do large farms have a negative impact on the environment?

    Dairy farms of all sizes are dedicated to doing their part to protect the environment. The environmental impact of a farm is determined more by correct management practises than by the number of cows on the farm. Large and small dairy farms are also looking for new ways to save energy, preserve water, and develop renewable energy sources. The environmental effect of producing a gallon of milk in 2017 was dramatically reduced, requiring 30% less water, 21% less land, and a 19% lower carbon footprint than it did in 2007. This was due to novel practises in cow comfort, superior feed and genetics, and modern barn design.
